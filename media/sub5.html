According to the "Korea Climate Change Evaluation Report 2020" recently published by the Ministry of Environment and the Korea Meteorolog ical Administration, the area of Arctic sea ice decreases by 3㎡ for every ton of carbon dioxide emitted. if 1,000 gigatons of carbon dioxide are emitted in the future, Arctic sea ice will almost disappear, and the timing is estimated to be around 2050, if 35 gigatons of carbon dioxide are currently maintained annually. using the UK Meteorological Agency's Hadley Center's state-of-the-art climate model, the research team estimated the high temperature of the Arctic during the last interglacial period, 116,000 to 130,000 years ago. during the interglacial period, temperatures in the Arctic were calculated to be 4 to 5 degrees higher than before the Industrial Revolution due to intense sunlight in high latitudes in the summer. At that time, the sea level was 6 to 9 meters higher than now. maria Vitoria Guarino, head of the study in Bath, said, "The temperature of the Arctic has been a long-standing mystery for scientists, a difficult challenge both technically and scientifically. Finnish daily newspapers have devised a way to visually see the melting rate of glaciers in order to promote the seriousness of climate change and urge action. Helsingin Sanomat, the largest newspaper in Northern Europe, recently distributed The Climate Crisis Font, a visualization of the severity of the climate crisis. The font is based on actual data from 1979 when sea ice data began to be measured and data predicted by 2050.
